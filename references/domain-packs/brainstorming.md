## Grill Lenses for Brainstorming / Strategy

| Lens | Perspective | Focus |
|------|------------|-------|
| **Critic** | System view | What assumption underlies each idea, and is it actually true? What evidence would disprove this? What has been conflated or left undefined? |
| **Alternative view** | Human reality | What is the strongest counter-argument? What competing approach would someone who disagrees propose? What does this idea miss or deprioritize that matters? |
| **Feasibility** | Technical depth | What would it actually take to execute this — time, cost, dependencies, skills? Is that realistic given current constraints? What is the path from idea to outcome, and where does it break? |

## How Forge Applies to Brainstorming

Brainstorming in Forge is not an open-ended idea dump. The goal is to produce **a decision or a ranked set of options with explicit assumptions** — an artifact the team can act on.

### Step 1 (Align Intent) — especially important here

Before generating ideas, answer:
- What specific decision or problem are we trying to resolve?
- What are the constraints (time, budget, team, technology, political)?
- What does "good enough" look like — what criteria will we use to evaluate options?

Without a clear decision frame, brainstorming produces ideas, not answers.

### Step 2 (Versioned Spec) — for brainstorming = a decision brief

The spec for a brainstorming session is a **decision brief** containing:
- The decision to be made (specific, bounded)
- Evaluation criteria (ranked by importance)
- Options generated (labeled, not yet evaluated)
- Key assumptions per option

### Step 3 (Adversarial Grill) — stress-test before committing

Run the three lenses on the leading option(s) before deciding. Common findings:
- Assumptions that are stated as facts but have not been verified
- Options that were not generated because they were uncomfortable
- Feasibility issues that make the leading option impractical

## Definition of Done — Brainstorming

A brainstorming session is done when:

- [ ] The decision to be made is clearly stated and agreed upon
- [ ] Options are documented with explicit assumptions per option
- [ ] Evaluation criteria are defined and used to score/rank options
- [ ] The top option(s) have been through the adversarial grill (at least the Critic and Feasibility lenses)
- [ ] Key assumptions are identified and marked as verified or unverified
- [ ] A decision (or a clear next step toward a decision) is documented
- [ ] Non-obvious options that were considered and rejected are noted (avoids re-litigating them)

---

## What to Verify

- Is the decision frame clear and agreed on by all stakeholders?
- Are the evaluation criteria explicit, ranked, and actually used to evaluate options?
- Has each leading option had its key assumptions checked against reality (not just assumed)?
- Is the documented decision specific enough to act on — not "we'll explore option A" but "we will do X by Y because of Z"?
- Are non-goals explicit? What is out of scope for this decision?

## Brainstorming Principles

- **Value question first.** Lead with the decision to be made, not with ideas. Ideas without a frame produce noise, not signal.
- **One focused round.** Gather all high-impact information in one pass. Avoid the infinite back-and-forth that delays the decision without improving it.
- **Explicit assumptions beat implicit ones.** Every option rests on assumptions. Name them so they can be verified or challenged.
- **Rejected options deserve a record.** Documenting why an option was rejected prevents it from being relitigated in every future meeting.
- **A decision is an artifact.** "We talked about it" is not done. A written decision brief is done.
